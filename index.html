Alphabet; Pronunciation; Quantity; Accent

1. With the exception that it has no w, the Latin alphabet is the same as the English, which has been borrowed from it.
2. The vowels, as in English, are a, e, i, o, u, and sometimes y. The other letters are consonants.

a. Modern printed texts often use one character (i) for both vocalic and consonantal i (i, j), and one for both vocalic and consonantal u (u, v). The Romans used no separate characters to distinguish the vocalic and consonantal uses of these letters.b. Between vowels within a word, j is regularly doubled in pronunciation though written once: major greater, pejor worse, ejus of him, hujus of this, cujus of whom, ajō say — pronounced as if spelled majjor, pejjor, ejjus, hujjus, cujjus, ajjō. This does not apply to j that was originally initial but falls between vowels through compounding: praejūdicō prejudge, bijugī chariot.c. When followed by i, as in compounds of jaciō throw, the j, though pronounced, is not usually written in the classical period: coniciō throw together, dēiciō cast down, ēiciō throw out, reiciō throw back. These are pronounced as if spelled conjiciō, dējiciō, ējiciō, rejiciō, and are so written in this text.
7. Doubled Consonants. All doubled consonants are sounded separately, not as one consonant as in English. Thus puella girl is pronounced pu‑el‑la, not pu-ell-a; so also er‑rō wander, stray, oc‑cu‑pō occupy.
8. Double Consonants. The double consonants are x and z: x is equivalent to ks or cs; z, a Greek letter, originally zd, may be pronounced as z in lazy.

Syllables

9. A Latin word has as many syllables as it has vowels and diphthongs: so‑ci‑us comrade, vo‑cā‑re to call, moe‑ni‑a walls, ac‑ci‑pi‑ō receive.
10. In dividing a Latin word into syllables, a single consonant goes with the following vowel: ca‑put head.
11. A consonant followed by l or r (except ll or rr) is usually pronounced with the l or r: pa‑trēs fathers, pū‑bli‑cus public.
12. In all other combinations of two or more consonants between vowels, the first consonant goes with the preceding vowel, the rest with the following vowel: her‑ba herb, mōn‑strō show, ar‑ma arms.
13. Exceptions. Compound words are divided according to their original elements: ab‑est he is absent, trāns‑eō I pass across.

Quantity

14. The quantity of a syllable is the relative amount of time taken to pronounce it. About twice as much time should be used for a long syllable as for a short one.
15. A syllable is long by nature when it contains a long vowel or a diphthong. It is long by position when its vowel is followed by two or more consonants pronounced separately (10–12), or by either double consonant x or z (8), or by j, which was regularly doubled in pronunciation (6 b). H never helps make a syllable long, and qu counts as a single consonant. Thus the first syllable of adhūc thus far and of aqua water is short.
16. Except under the metrical accent (394), a final syllable ending in a short vowel regularly stays short before a word beginning with two consonants or a double consonant.
17. If a consonant followed by l or r comes after a short vowel, the syllable with the short vowel is said to be common — it may be either long or short, at the speaker’s pleasure.
18. Note: This is because the l and r blend so easily with the preceding consonant that the combination takes scarcely more time than a single consonant. When the l or r is pronounced separately from the preceding consonant (as may always be done), more time is required and the preceding syllable is treated as long.
19. Observe that the vowel in a long syllable may be either long or short, and is pronounced accordingly. Thus in errō wander, captō seize, vertō turn, nox night, the first vowel is short and must be so pronounced (per 4), but the syllable is long and must take more time (per 14, 15).
20. A vowel is regularly short before another vowel, or before h: aes‑tu‑ō boil, de‑us god, tra‑hō draw.
a. This does not apply to Greek words in Latin, such as ā‑ēr air, Ī‑xī‑ōn Ixion (a proper name).
21. A vowel is regularly short before nt or nd. Observe that the syllable in this case is long.
22. A vowel is regularly short before any final consonant except s.
a. Some monosyllables ending in l, r, n, and c have a long vowel: sōl sun, pār equal, nōn not, sīc so.
23. A vowel is regularly long before ns, nf, nx, and nct.
24. Diphthongs and vowels derived from diphthongs or contracted from other vowels are regularly long.

Accent

25. Words of two syllables have the accent (stress) on the first: á‑mō love.
a. The last syllable of a word is the ultima (ultimus last); the next-to-last is the penult (paene almost + ultimus last); the one before that is the antepenult (ante before + paene, ultimus).
26. Words of more than two syllables (polysyllabic words) accent the penult when it is long, the antepenult when the penult is short or common (17): aes‑tu‑á‑re boil, á‑ni‑mus mind.
27. Enclitics. Certain words are not written separately but appended to the word they follow. The most common are ‑que and, ‑ve or, and ‑ne (sign of a question). When appended to a word accented on the antepenult, they add an accent to the ultima: forí­bus′que and from the doorway. When appended to a two-syllable word with a long ultima, the accent shifts to the ultima: amō′que and I love; but bél′laque and wars, í′taque and so.

Pronunciation of Latin Words in English

28. Latin words and phrases used in English are pronounced by the ordinary English system. For example, in alumnī the final ī is pronounced as i in five, and the u as u in bum; in the phrase via Chicago, v as in vine and i as in vine; in vōx populī the voice of the people, v as in voice, ō as in ox, u as in due, ī as in die.

Number

29. Latin has two numbers: the singular, denoting one; the plural, denoting more than one. For the poetic plural, see 243.

Third Declension

39. The stem ends in a consonant or in the close (high) vowels i and ū. The stems are divided by their last letter, the stem characteristic:
I. Consonant Stems
1.Liquid stems, ending in l, m, n, r.
2.Sibilant stems, ending in ‑s.
3. Mute stems a. ending in b, p. b. ending in g, c. c. ending in d, t.
II. i-Stems
Consonant types. Mutes (or stops) are made by closing the breath completely and then releasing it: b, p (lips), d, t (tongue and teeth), g, c (throat). Liquids are l, m, n, r, flowing sounds that can be held and drawn out. Sibilants are hissing sounds; in Latin the only one is s.
40. 1. The nominative singular (m. & f.) case ending is ‑s, which is dropped after l, n, r, s, and combines with c or g to form x. In the other cases the endings are added to the unchanged stem. The vocative of the third declension is always like the nominative.
2. Neuters form the nominative without a case ending; the accusative and vocative in both numbers are like the nominative. The nominative plural ends in ‑a.

Comparison of Adjectives

83. The Degrees of Comparison are: positive, comparative, and superlative.
84. The Comparative (the degree of an adjective expressing a greater amount of a quality, English ‘-er’ or ‘more’, used in comparing two things) is formed by adding to the consonant stems the endings ‑ior (m. & f.) and ‑ius (n.).
85. The Superlative (the degree of an adjective expressing the greatest amount of a quality, English ‘-est’ or ‘most’) is formed by adding to the consonant stems the endings ‑issimus, ‑a, ‑um (earlier ‑issumus).
86. Vowel stems drop their characteristic vowel before forming the comparative and superlative.

Adverbs

93. Most adverbs are either oblique cases (cases other than the nominative) or shortened forms of oblique cases of adjectival, nominal, or pronominal stems.
94. The cases from which they are derived are principally the accusative and the ablative.

Adverbs Formed from Adjectives

95. Many adverbs are formed from the ablative of adjectives in ‑us: tūtō safely, prīmō at first.
a. Many adverbs are formed from an old ablative form (‑ē) of adjectives in ‑us and ‑er: altē loftily, pulchrē beautifully.b. Many adverbs are formed from the accusative singular neuter of adjectival and pronominal stems: multum much, facile easily. This is true of all comparatives: facilius more easily. To the comparatives belong also magis more, nimis too.c. Adverbs are regularly formed from third-declension adjectives and participles by adding ‑ter (‑iter) to the stem; stems in nt drop the t, and stems in c or g insert the connecting vowel i: fortiter bravely, ferōciter wildly, prūdenter prudently.

102. A Pronoun is a word that may be substituted for a noun. Latin pronouns fall into six classes: personal (including reflexive), demonstrative, intensive, relative, interrogative, and indefinite.

The Verb

115. The inflection given to the verbal stem is called conjugation and expresses:
1. Person: First, Second, Third.2. Voice: Active, Passive, Middle.a. The active voice denotes that the action proceeds from the subject: parō I prepare. The passive voice denotes that the subject receives the action: paror I am prepared. The middle voice has passive form and denotes the subject as acting for or upon itself (reflexive): paror I prepare (for) myself. This is chiefly a poetic usage.3. Tense: Present, Imperfect, Future, Perfect, Pluperfect, Future Perfect.4. Mood: Indicative, Subjunctive, Imperative.a. The indicative is the mood of fact: parō I prepare. The subjunctive is the mood of wish, command, or qualified statement: parem may I prepare, I may prepare; paret may he prepare, let him prepare; sī paret if he should prepare. The imperative is the mood of command: parā prepare!
Finite and Infinite Forms of the Verb

A Latin verb has two broad kinds of forms. Finite forms are limited (“finite”) by a personal ending: they fix the action to a person and number (I, you, he; we, you, they) and carry a tense, mood, and voice. Vocat, he calls, is finite — it has a built-in subject. These are the forms set out in the conjugation tables that follow.

The infinite forms (Pharr’s “infinite parts of the verb,” 117) are not limited in that way — “infinite” here means unbounded by person. Having no personal ending, they name no subject of their own. Instead each turns the verb into a noun or an adjective, while keeping the verb’s sense of action, tense, and voice. There are five:

A verb can become a noun in three ways. The infinitive names the action as a thing — English “to” plus a verb (amāre, to love). The gerund is the action treated as a declinable noun, like the English “-ing” noun in “the art of loving” (amandī, of loving). The supine is a rare noun surviving in just two forms, used to express purpose (‑um) or respect (‑ū).

A verb can become an adjective in two ways. The participle describes a noun while keeping the verb’s action and time — a verbal adjective (amāns, loving; amātus, having been loved). The gerundive is a verbal adjective of obligation — what ought to be done (amandus, to be loved).

Each of these is built on one of the three stems given in 116, and each agrees or is used according to its kind: the noun-forms take cases, the adjective-forms agree with a noun in gender, number, and case.

126. 1. There are two systems of conjugation, the vowel and the consonant. The consonant system is confined to a small class. The vowel system comprises four conjugations, distinguished by the vowel characteristics of the present stem — ā, ē, e, ī — found by dropping re from the present infinitive active. The consonant before the short vowel stem characteristic is the consonant stem characteristic.

Formation of the Tenses

127. The tenses are formed by adding the personal endings to the various stems, either directly or by means of certain tense signs, as shown in the paradigms.
128. While no fully practical rules can be given, observe that:
1. The second person singular imperative active is the same as the present stem.2. The imperfect subjunctive may be formed from the present infinitive active by adding ‑m, etc. (active) and ‑r, etc. (passive).3. The second person singular imperative passive, and the second person singular present passive in ‑re, are the same as the present infinitive active. Hence ‑ris is preferred in the present indicative passive, to avoid confusion.4. The present subjunctive active and future indicative active in the third and fourth conjugations are alike in the first person singular.5. The future perfect indicative active and the perfect subjunctive active differ only in the first person singular, except for the quantity of the i.6. Euphonic changes sometimes occur in the consonant stem characteristic in the perfect and participial stem. b before s and t becomes p; g and qu before t become c; c, g, qu with s become x; t and d before s are assimilated, then sometimes dropped: scrībō, scrīpsī, scrīptus; legō, lēctus; coquō, coctus; dīcō, dīxī; jungō, jūnxī; coquō, coxī; edō, ēsus; cēdō, cessī; mittō, mīsī, missus.

Principal Rules of Syntax

Agreement

236. A verb agrees with its subject in number and person: ego vocō, I call; tū vocās, you call.
a. A collective noun may take its predicate (everything the sentence says about the subject, here the verb) in the plural: pars veniunt, part come.b. The nearer subject is preferred to the more remote: mīlitēs et dux vēnit, the leader and his troops came.c. The copula (the linking verb, e.g. est) may agree in number with the predicate noun (a noun joined to the subject by a linking verb such as est, standing in the same case): amantium īrae amōris integrātiō est, lovers’ quarrels are love’s renewal. Compare “the wages of sin is death.”
237. The adjective agrees with its noun in gender, number, and case: tālis vir, such a man; tālēs virī, such men; tālia dōna, such gifts.
238. The common attribute of two or more nouns generally agrees with the nearest, rather than the most important: cūncta maria terraeque patēbant, all seas and lands lay open.
239. The common predicate attribute of two or more subjects is usually plural: pater et māter mortuī sunt, father and mother are dead; but may agree with the nearest or most important: Caesar mortuus est et Jūlia, Caesar is dead, and Julia also.
a. When the subjects differ in person, the predicate takes the first in preference to the second, the second in preference to the third.b. When the subjects differ in gender, the predicate attribute takes the nearest gender or the strongest (masculine being strongest of things with life, neuter of those without): pater et māter mortuī sunt, father and mother are dead; mūrus et porta dē caelō tācta erant, wall and gate had been struck by lightning.
240. The predicate substantive (noun) agrees with its subject in case: ille vir erat dux, that man was leader.
a. The pronoun used as subject is commonly attracted into the gender of the predicate: negat Epicūrus; hoc enim vestrum lūmen est, Epicurus says No; for he is your great light!
241. The appositive (a noun set beside another that names the same thing, standing in the same case) agrees with its subject in case; if possible, also in number and person: Aenēās, pius dux, Aeneas, the devoted leader; Aenēās et Dīdō, superbī ducēs, Aeneas and Dido, proud leaders.
242. The relative pronoun agrees with its antecedent in gender, number, and person: vir quī adest, the man who is present; dōna quae adsunt, the gifts which are here; ego quī hoc dīcō, I who say this.
a. Occasionally the antecedent is attracted into the case of the relative: urbem quam statuō vestra est, the city which I am founding is yours.
243. Poetic Plural. The plural is often used for the singular, especially in poetry, to generalize, for metrical reasons, or for rhetorical effect: īrae, wrath, wrathful passions; fortitūdinēs, gallant actions; ōra, face, features; scēptra, scepter; silentia, silence.
a. Plural of Modesty. The plural of the first person is sometimes used for the singular: dīximus multa, we (I) have spoken much. Similarly nōs in all its cases for ego, and noster in all its forms for meus. In English this is sometimes called the “editorial we.”
244. Disproportion is indicated by the comparative with quam prō than for, quam ut than that, quam quī than who: minor caedēs quam prō tantā victōriā fuit, the loss was (too) small for so great a victory; major sum quam cui fortūna nocēre possit, I am too great for fortune possibly to hurt me.
245. In comparing two qualities, use either magis quam with the positive — disertus magis est quam sapiēns, he is eloquent rather than wise — or a double comparative: acūtiōrem sē quam ōrnātiōrem vult, he wishes to be acute rather than ornate.
246. Superlatives denoting order and sequence are often used partitively, usually preceding their nouns: summa aqua, the surface of the water; summus mōns, the top of the mountain; prīmō vēre, in the beginning of spring. Similarly in mediā urbe, in the midst of the city; reliqua, cētera Graecia, the rest of Greece.

Pronouns

247. Since the verb form indicates its person, the nominative of the personal pronouns is used only for emphasis or contrast: ego rēgēs ējēcī, vōs tyrannōs intrōdūxistī, I drove out kings; you bring in tyrants.
a. The forms meī, tuī, nostrī, vestrī are used as objective genitives; nostrum and vestrum as partitive genitives (284, 286).
248. The reflexive is used regularly when reference is made to the grammatical subject: ipse sē quisque dīligit, quod sibi quisque cārus est, every one loves himself, because every one is dear to himself.
a. The reflexive is sometimes used of the logical subject: ferunt sua flāmina classem, their own (favorable) breezes waft the fleet.b. The reflexive is used of the principal subject when reference is made to its thought or will, especially in indirect discourse or substantive volitive clauses (clauses of desire): sentit animus sē vī suā movērī, the mind feels that it moves by its own force; ā mē petīvit ut sēcum essem, he asked me to be with him.
249. The possessive adjective is used instead of the genitive of the first- and second-person pronouns: socius meus, a comrade of mine; tēlum tuum, a weapon of yours; amor meus, my love (which I feel); spēs tua, your hope (which you have).
a. The appositive to a possessive adjective is in the genitive: urbs mea ūnīus operā fuit salva, the city was saved by my exertions alone.

Adjectives for Adverbs

250. With words of inclination and disinclination, knowledge and ignorance, order and position, time and season, the adjective is commonly used for the adverb: id faciō volēns, I do this willing(ly).

Special Use of Moods

The English subjunctive is still in there

Latin’s subjunctive mood handles wishes, hypotheticals, and “would/might” situations — the things not simply being stated as fact, which is the job of the indicative. English does the same, but its subjunctive has worn down to a few odd-looking verb forms. Compare:

Indicative (fact)Subjunctive (not a fact)
He is here.I wish he were here.
She is told first.I insist that she be told first.
It was so.Would that it were so.
I was you. (ungrammatical)If I were you…

Each right-hand form veers off the ordinary one to mark something wished-for or hypothetical. Latin marks that same swerve far more often, and with its own endings — so as the sections below pile up may, might, should, would, read them as this swerve away from fact, not as random word choices.

The mood also gets pulled into many subordinate clauses, and that is where its name comes from: subjunctive is literally “joined-underneath” (sub‑ + iungō), the mood of the clause hanging under a main verb. So when a cum clause or an indirect question turns subjunctive, it isn’t always signaling unreality — sometimes it’s just marking “this clause is subordinate.”

251. The Indicative, not the subjunctive, is commonly used to express possibility, power, obligation, and necessity: possum persequī permulta oblectāmenta rērum rūsticārum, I might rehearse very many delights of country life; ad mortem tē dūcī oportēbat, you ought to have been led to execution.
252. The Potential Subjunctive. The present or perfect subjunctive may express possibility in present or future: tē superesse velim, I should like for you to survive; Platōnem nōn nimis valdē laudāverīs, you can’t praise Plato too much; the imperfect subjunctive expresses past possibility: crēderēs victōs, you would (might) have thought them beaten.
253. The Optative Subjunctive. The subjunctive, sometimes with utinam would that, is often used to express a wish. The negative is nē: valeās, farewell!; nē veniant, may they not come!; utinam nē nātus essem, would that I had not been born!; utinam vīveret frāter, would that my brother were alive!
254. The Volitive Subjunctive. The subjunctive may express will, asseveration, command, or concession: stet haec urbs, may this city continue to stand; moriar sī magis gaudērem, may I die if I could be more glad; amēmus patriam, let us love our country; sit deus, granted that he is a god. This subjunctive is often called jussive, hortatory, or concessive, according as it expresses a command, an exhortation, or a concession.
255. The present imperative looks to immediate, the future imperative to contingent, fulfillment: haec dīcite vestrō rēgī, tell this to your king; cōnsulēs nēminī pārentō, the consuls shall obey no one.
256. The negative of the imperative is regularly nōlī with the infinitive: nōlī fugere, don’t flee!; sometimes nē or cavē (nē) with the present or perfect subjunctive: cavē (nē) faciās, don’t do it!; nē mortem timeās (or timuerītis), don’t fear death!
a. In poetry nē is often found with the imperative: nē timē, fear not!; nē fuge, don’t run!

Infinitives

257. The Historical Infinitive. The present infinitive, with subject in the nominative, is sometimes used for a rapid sequence of events: hinc Ulixēs terrēre, henceforth Ulysses began to frighten (me).
258. The Infinitive as Neuter Subject. The infinitive, with or without a subject, may be treated as a neuter subject, object, or predicate: dulce et decōrum est prō patriā morī, it is sweet and glorious to die for one’s fatherland; turpe est vincī, it is shameful to be conquered; scit vincere, he knows how to conquer; vīvere est cōgitāre, to live is to think.
259. The Complementary Infinitive. The infinitive is used with verbs of will, desire, power, duty, habit, inclination, resolve, continuance, purpose, etc.: cupit morī, he wishes to die; dēbet haec vulnera patī, he ought to suffer these wounds.
260. The Infinitive as Object. The accusative with infinitive is used as the object of verbs of emotion, will, and desire: tē vēnisse gaudeō, I rejoice that you have come; mē dīcere vult, he wishes me to speak.
261. The Infinitive of Purpose. In poetry the infinitive often expresses purpose: nōn populāre vēnimus, we have not come to pillage.
262. The Infinitive of Exclamation. The infinitive with accusative subject is used in exclamation or exclamatory questions: mē dēsistere, what! I desist?
263. The Infinitive of Indirect Statement (Indirect Discourse). The infinitive is used after verbs of saying, showing, believing, and perceiving to express an indirect statement. The present infinitive expresses action contemporary with the governing verb; the perfect, action prior to it; the future, action later: dīcit tē errāre, he says that you are wrong; dīcēbat tē errāre, he was saying that you were wrong; dīcēbat tē errāsse, he was saying that you had been wrong.
a. The future infinitive of sum followed by ut and the subjunctive is used in indirect statement of future action when the verb has no future participle: dīcit fore (futūrum esse) ut metuās, he says that you will fear.
264. The Infinitive with Nouns. The poets and later prose writers use the infinitive with nouns of attention or opportunity: amor cāsūs cognōscere nostrōs, desire to know our misfortunes; adfectāre potestās, opportunity to seize.
265. The Infinitive with Adjectives. The infinitive is used with many adjectives and with participles of adjectival force: nescia vincī pectora, hearts not knowing how to yield; certa morī, determined to die.

Gerunds and Gerundives

266. The genitive of the gerund or gerundive is used chiefly after nouns and adjectives that require a complement: sapientia ars vīvendī putanda est, philosophy is to be considered the art of living; nūlla spēs plācandī deī, no hope of appeasing god; cupidus maledīcendī, eager to abuse.
267. The dative of the gerund or gerundive is used mainly in post-classical Latin after words of fitness and function, capacity and adaptation, and to express purpose: tēla apta mittendō, weapons suitable for hurling; comitia cōnsulibus creandīs, elections for nominating consuls.
268. The accusative of the gerund or gerundive is used with ad to express purpose: ad bellandum vēnimus, we come to make war.
a. The accusative of the gerundive agrees with the direct object of verbs of giving, sending, and leaving, to indicate purpose: id mihi servandum dedit, he gave it to me to keep; Cōnōn mūrōs reficiendōs cūrat, Conon has the walls rebuilt; patriam dīripiendam relīquimus, we have left our country to be plundered.
269. The ablative of the gerund or gerundive denotes means and cause, rarely manner: ūnus homō nōbīs cūnctandō restituit rem, one man by delaying raised our cause again.

Supines

270. The supine in ‑um (accusative) is used chiefly after verbs of motion to express purpose: veniunt spectātum, they come to see.
271. The supine in ‑ū (ablative) is used chiefly with adjectives to indicate respect: mīrābile dictū, wonderful to relate; mīrābile vīsū, wonderful to see.

Participles

272. The Present Participle denotes continuance; the perfect participle denotes completion at the time of the principal verb.
273. The participle is used after verbs of perception and representation to express the object’s actual condition at the time: Catōnem vīdī in bibliothēcā sedentem multīs circumfūsum librīs, I saw Cato sitting in the library with an ocean of books about him; illam audīvī furtīvā vōce loquentem, I heard her talking in a stealthy tone.
274. The Future Participle is used in poetry and occasionally in prose to express desire or purpose: fabricāta est māchina īnspectūra domōs ventūraque dēsuper urbī, it has been built as an engine of war, to spy into our homes and come down upon the city from above.
275. The Perfect Participle passive is used after verbs of causation and desire, to denote that entire fulfillment is demanded or desired: sī quī voluptātibus dūcuntur, missōs faciant honōrēs, if any are led captive by sensual pleasures, let them give up honors (at once and forever).

Cases

Nominative

276. The subject of a finite verb is in the nominative: urbs stat, the city is standing.
277. The subject of a historical infinitive is in the nominative: Ulixēs terrēre, Ulysses began to terrorize (me).
278. The predicate of a finite form of sum, or of a verb of seeming or becoming, or of the passive of a verb of making, choosing, showing, thinking, or calling, is in the nominative: pater est rēx, his father is king; pater fit rēx, his father becomes king; pater vocātur rēx, his father is called king.
279. When an active verb of saying, showing, believing, or perceiving is made passive, the accusative subject of the infinitive may become the nominative subject of the leading verb: urbs dīcitur magna fuisse, the city is said to have been great. Compare dīcunt urbem fuisse magnam, they say that the city was great.

Genitive

280. Genitive of Material. The genitive may denote the material of which a thing consists: flūmina lactis, rivers of milk. see 324 for the ablative of material.
281. The Appositional Genitive is used with vōx, nōmen, verbum, rēs, urbs, etc.: nōmen amīcitiae, the name of friendship; urbem Patavī, the city of Patavium.
282. The Epexegetical Genitive (genitive of explanation) is used after genus, vitium, culpa, etc.: virtūtēs continentiae, gravitātis, jūstitiae, fideī, the virtues of self-control, earnestness, justice, honor.
283. The Possessive Genitive denotes possession: domus rēgis, the palace of the king, the king’s palace.
284. The Subjective Genitive denotes the subject of the action; the Objective Genitive denotes its object: metus hostium, the fear of the enemy — either (1) the fear the enemy feel (subjective), or (2) the fear felt toward the enemy (objective). Examples: cupīdō glōriae, desire for glory; tempestātum potentem, ruling the storms; memorem vestrī, oblītum suī, mindful of you, forgetful of himself.
285. Genitive of Quality. The genitive with an adjective may describe a person or thing: homō maximī corporis, a man of gigantic size. Compare the ablative of quality (330).
a. The genitive of quality or the possessive genitive may be used as a predicate: id virtūtis est, that is a mark of virtue; hujus erō vīvus, mortuus hujus erō, hers I shall be living; dead, hers shall I be; haec domus est patris meī, this house is my father’s.
286. The Partitive Genitive stands for the whole to which a part belongs: pars hominum, part of the men; maximus omnium, greatest of all.
287. Genitive with Special Adjectives and Verbs. Adjectives and verbs of fullness and want, knowledge and ignorance, desire and disgust, participation and power may take the genitive: plēnus labōris, full of toil; egēnus omnium, in need of everything; cōnscius rēctī, conscious of right; ignārus malī, ignorant of misfortune; cupidus aurī, desirous of gold; dīligēns vērī, careful (a lover) of the truth; capāx imperī, capable of empire; implentur veteris Bacchī, they fill themselves with old wine.
288. Genitive with Verbs. Verbs of reminding, remembering, and forgetting usually take the genitive: meminit malōrum, oblītus est bonōrum, he remembers the evil, forgets the good.
a. Sometimes these verbs take the accusative, especially of things: haec ōlim meminisse juvābit, to remember these things will one day be a pleasure.
289. Verbs of Emotion take the genitive: miserēre animī nōn digna ferentis, pity a soul enduring what it does not deserve.
290. Impersonal verbs (verbs used only in the third-person singular with no real subject, like English ‘it rains’ or ‘it is allowed’) of emotion take the accusative of the person who feels and the genitive of the exciting cause: tuī mē miseret, meī piget, I pity you, I loathe myself.
291. Verbs of accusing, convicting, condemning, and acquitting take the genitive of the charge: accūsātus est prōditiōnis, he was accused of treason; damnātus est caedis, he was convicted of murder; absolūtus est crīminis, he was acquitted of the charge.
292. Genitive of Value. Verbs of rating and buying take the genitive of general value: parvī existimāre, to consider of small account; ēmit equōs tantī quantī Caesar voluit, he bought the horses at the price Caesar wanted.
293. Interest and rēfert take the genitive of the person, rarely of the thing, concerned: interest omnium rēctē facere, it is to the interest of all to do right; rēfert omnium, it concerns everybody.
a. Sometimes the ablative singular feminine of the possessive adjective is used: meā interest, meā rēfert, I am concerned.
294. Genitive of Respect. The genitive is used with various adjectives to denote the respect in which a thing is true: aeger animī, sick at heart.

Dative

295. The Indirect Object is put in the dative: aurum hominī dat, he gives gold to the man.
296. Some verbs of giving take either the dative and accusative, or the accusative and ablative: hominī aurum dōnat, he presents the gold to the man; hominem aurō dōnat, he presents the man with gold.
297. Dative with Special Verbs. Many verbs meaning favor, help, trust, bid and forbid, believe, persuade, obey, serve, threaten, pardon, spare, join, and contend take the dative: invideō nēminī, I envy no one; cēdit fortūnae, he yields to fortune; id mihi placet, that pleases me; pārēmus ducī, we obey our leader; crēdit hominī, he trusts the man.
298. Dative with Compounds. Many intransitive verbs compounded with ad, ante, con, in, inter, ob, post, prae, sub, and super may take a dative: accēdō equō, I approach the horse; antecellit omnibus, he excels all; nox incubat marī, night broods over the sea; piger ipse sibi obstat, the lazy man stands in his own way.
a. Transitive verbs so compounded also take an accusative: mē vestrīs ōrīs deus appulit, god drove me to your shores.
299. Dative of Possession. The dative is used with esse to denote possession: est ager nōbīs, we have a field.
300. The Ethical Dative is used only of the personal pronouns: ecce tibi homō, here’s your man!; tibi bellum geret, he shall wage war (for you, let me tell you).
301. The Dative of Reference is used of the person interested or concerned, or to whom a statement is referred: deō altāria fūmant, the altars smoke in honor of the god; pulchra est multīs, she is beautiful to many (in the eyes of many); est urbe ēgressīs tumulus, as you go out of town there is a mound. Also called the dative of interest, advantage, and disadvantage.
302. The Dative of Agent is used with a passive verb, especially the gerundive: id faciendum mihi, this must be done by me, I must do this; vetor fātīs, I am forbidden by the fates; urbs capta est mihi, the city was captured by me.
303. Dative of Purpose. The dative may denote the object for which (purpose): pars optāre locum tēctō, part were choosing a site for a home.
a. The dative of purpose is often combined with the dative of reference (301): auxiliō iīs fuit, he was a help to them; id erit cūrae mihi, that shall be my care.
304. Dative with Adjectives. Adjectives of friendliness, fullness, likeness, nearness, and their opposites take the dative: similis est hominī, he is like a man; homō amīcus est mihi, the man is friendly to me; id erit ūtile omnibus, this will be useful to all; proximus sum egomet mihi, myself am nearest to me.
305. Dative of Separation. Many verbs of warding off, robbing, ridding, depriving, and separation take a dative, especially in poetry: ēripiēs mihi hunc errōrem, you will rid me of this mistake; silicī scintillam excūdit, he strikes a spark from the flint.
306. Dative of Direction. In poetry the place to which, or limit of motion, is often expressed by the dative: it clāmor caelō, a shout goes to heaven; multōs dēmittimus Orcō, we send many to Hades.

Accusative

307. The Direct Object of active transitive verbs is in the accusative and may denote the object effected or affected: bellum gerunt, they wage war; condidit urbem, he founds the city; capit urbem, he captures the city; rēx dūcit hominēs, the king leads the men.
308. Accusative with Compounds. Many intransitive verbs, mostly of motion, compounded with ad, ante, circum, con, in, inter, ob, per, praeter, sub, subter, super, and trāns take the accusative: adit urbem, he approaches the city; poenam subit, he submits to punishment.
a. Transitive verbs thus compounded may have two accusatives: exercitum flūmen trājēcit, he threw his army across the river.
309. In poetry the passive is often used in a reflexive or middle sense: fertur in hostīs, he charges upon the enemy.
a. So used, the verb may take an accusative as direct object: ferrum cingitur, he girds on the steel; sinūs collēcta, having gathered her robes; tūnsae pectora, beating their breasts; īnsternor umerōs, I spread over my shoulders.
310. The accusative and ablative of certain adjectives are used adverbially: multum jactātus, much tossed; prīmō, at first. see 93–94.
311. Accusative of Respect. In poetry the accusative often denotes the part concerned: nūda genū, with knee bare; ōs umerōsque deō similis, like a god in face and shoulders.
312. Accusative with Prepositions. The accusative is used with many prepositions (little words placed before a noun to show a relation such as place or direction, like English ‘to’ or ‘with’), the most important being:
ante, apud, ad, adversum
circum, cis, ob, trāns, secundum
penes, pōne, prope, per
post, and all in ‑ā and ‑ter.footnote
Observe the metrical form of these four lines.
313. Cognate Accusative. Intransitive verbs may take an accusative of similar form or meaning: dum vītam vīvās, as long as you live; somnium somniāvī, I dreamed a dream.
314. Accusative of Extent. The accusative may express extent in space, time, or degree: tumulum centum pedēs altum, a mound one hundred feet high; fuit rēx decem annōs, he was king ten years; sī mē amās tantum quantum tē amō, if you love me as I love you.
315. Place Whither (place to which) is regularly denoted by the accusative with ad or in: vēnit ad Ītaliam, he came to Italy.
a. The names of towns, small islands, domus, and rūs do not ordinarily take the preposition: vēnit Rōmam, he came to Rome; pater, venī domum, father, come home!; fugiunt rūs, they flee to the country.b. In poetry the preposition is often omitted: Ītaliam Lāvīnaque vēnit lītora, he came to Italy and the Lavinian shores.
316. Two Accusatives. Verbs meaning to inquire, require, teach, and conceal take two accusatives, one of the person, one of the thing: quis tē illud docuit, who taught you that?; Mīlēsiōs nāvem poposcit, he demanded a ship of the Milesians; quid mē istud rogās, why do you ask me that?
a. Verbs of naming, making, taking, choosing, and showing take two accusatives of the same person or thing: vocant urbem Rōmam, they call the city Rome; illum rēgem faciunt, they make him king.
317. The Subject of the Infinitive is regularly in the accusative: vult mē dīcere, he wishes me to speak.
a. A predicate noun in such an infinitive phrase agrees with the subject: volunt eum rēgem esse, they wish him to be king.
318. Accusative of Exclamation. The accusative may be used in exclamations: mē miserum, poor me!; prō deum fidem, for heaven’s sake!

Ablative

319. Place Where is denoted by the ablative, usually with in: in altō et in terrīs, on sea and on land.
a. With names of towns and small islands the locative expresses place where: Rōmae, at Rome. see 345.b. In poetry the preposition is often omitted: terrā marīque, on land and sea; Ītaliā, in Italy; lītore, on the shore; pectore, in his heart.
320. Place Whence is denoted by the ablative, usually with ex, dē, or ab: ex marī, out of the sea.
a. With names of towns, small islands, domō from home, and rūre from the country, the preposition is regularly omitted: Karthāgine, from Carthage.b. In poetry the preposition is often omitted: venit (ex) Ītaliā, he comes from Italy.
321. Ablative of Accompaniment. Accompaniment is denoted by the ablative with cum: venit cum sociīs, he comes with his comrades.
a. With mē, tē, sē, nōbīs, vōbīs, quō, quā, quibus the preposition cum is regularly appended: mēcum, with me; tēcum, with you; sēcum, with him(self), with them(selves).
322. Time When or Within Which is denoted by the ablative: urbem cēpit nocte, he captured the city at night; nocte pluit tōtā, it rains all night.
323. Origin (source or descent) is denoted by the ablative with or without ex or dē: nātus deā, born of a goddess; ex mē atque ex hōc nātus es, you are his son and mine.
324. Ablative of Material. Material is denoted by the ablative, usually with ex: domus facta ex saxō, ex ferrō, a house made of stone, of iron; aere clipeus, a shield of bronze. see 280 for the genitive of material.
325. Ablative of Respect. The respect in which a verb, adjective, or noun is to be taken is denoted by the ablative: sunt quīdam hominēs nōn rē sed nōmine, some people are human beings not in fact but in name only.
326. Ablative of Accordance. The ablative, usually with dē or ex, expresses that in accordance with which a thing is done or judged: ex senātūs cōnsultō, in accordance with the decree of the senate.
a. With certain words, accordance is usually expressed by the ablative without a preposition: cōnsuētūdine suā, according to his custom; tuō cōnsiliō, in accordance with your plan; meā sententiā, in my opinion.
327. Ablative with Comparatives. Comparatives without quam are followed by the ablative: exēgī monumentum aere perennius, I have erected a monument more enduring than bronze; Ō mātre pulchrā fīlia pulchrior, O daughter fairer than a mother fair!
328. Ablative of Manner. Manner is denoted by the ablative, regularly with an adjective or with cum: cum virtūte vīvere, to live virtuously; id fēcit magnā cum cūrā, he did it very carefully.
a. The simple ablative of certain nouns may denote manner: cāsū, by chance; vī, by force.
329. Ablative of Attendant Circumstance. Attendant circumstance, situation, or result may be expressed by the ablative, usually with a modifier and without a preposition, but sometimes with cum: magnō intervallō, at a great distance; frequentissimō senātū, at a crowded meeting of the senate; imperiō suō, under his full power; clāmōre, with shouting; cum tuā perniciē, to your destruction; magnō (cum) dolōre omnium, to the great grief of all.
330. Ablative of Quality. Quality or description is denoted by the ablative, regularly with an adjective: statūrā fuit humilī, he was of low stature. see 285 for the genitive of quality.
331. Ablative of Means. Means or instrument is denoted by the ablative, without a preposition: clārē videō oculīs, I see clearly with my eyes; pugnābant armīs, they fought with arms.
332. Ablative of Cause. Cause may be expressed by the ablative without a preposition: ōdērunt peccāre bonī virtūtis amōre, the good hate to sin from love of virtue.
333. Ablative of Agent. The agent is denoted by the ablative with ā (ab): urbs capta est ab illō duce, the city was captured by that leader.
334. The Standard of Measurement is denoted by the ablative: magnōs hominēs virtūte mētimur, nōn fortūnā, we measure great men by worth, not by fortune.
335. Measure of Difference is put in the ablative: sōl multīs partibus est major quam terra ūniversa, the sun is many parts (a great deal) larger than the whole earth.
336. Ablative of Price. Definite price is put in the ablative: ēmit morte immortālitātem, he purchased deathlessness with death.
a. General value is expressed by the genitive. see 292.
337. Ablative with Adjectives. The ablative is used with dignus, indignus, frētus, contentus, and laetus, and with adjectives of fullness and abundance: contentus hōc equō, content with this horse; dignus poenā, worthy of punishment; indignus poenā, unworthy of punishment.
a. Also the verb dignor deem worthy takes the ablative in poetry and later prose: haud tālī mē dignor honōre, I do not deem myself worthy of such honor.
338. The Ablative of Route denotes the way by which: prōvehimur pelagō, we sail forth over the sea.
339. Ablative with Prepositions. The ablative is used with many prepositions, the most important being:
abs (ab, ā), cum, cōram, dē
prae, prō, sine, ex (or ē).
340. Ablative of Separation. Separation is expressed by the ablative, with or without a preposition, especially with verbs of depriving, freedom, and want: sē prīvāvit oculīs, he deprived himself of his eyes; omnibus egēre rēbus, to be in need of everything; tē ab eō līberō, I free you from him.
a. The ablative of separation is also used with aliēnus and adjectives of freedom and want: aliēnum mājestāte deōrum, inconsistent with the dignity of the gods; līber cūrā, free from care.
341. The ablative is used with opus and ūsus: opus est mihi aurō, I need gold; ūsus est dictīs bonīs, there is need of kind words.
342. Ablative with Special Verbs. Ūtor, fruor, fungor, potior, vescor, and their compounds take the ablative: hīs vōcibus ūsa est, she spoke (used) these words; abūsus est nostrā patientiā, he used up our patience; fruimur lūce vītae, we enjoy the light of life; fungor officiō, I am performing my duty; potītur victōriā, he gains the victory; lacte vescuntur, they live on milk.
343. Ablative Absolute. The ablative, combined with a participle, adjective, or noun, may express the circumstances of an act: duce victō, abeunt, the leader having been conquered, they depart; urbe captā, hominēs redeunt, the city having been captured, the men return.
a. The ablative absolute may have an accessory idea of time, cause, or condition: Caesare cōnsule haec lēx lāta est, this law was passed in Caesar’s consulship.
344. Vocative. The vocative, with or without ō, O, is used in addressing a person or thing: Mūsa, O Muse!; Aeole, O Aeolus!; ō rēgīna, O queen!

Locative

345. Names of towns and small islands of the first and second declensions, humus, domus, and rūs, are put in the locative of the place where: Rōmae, at Rome; Crētae, in Crete; humī, on the ground; domī, at home.
a. In poetry the names of countries and large islands are sometimes found in the locative: Libyae, in Libya.

Adverbs

346. Adverbs qualify verbs, adjectives, and other adverbs: male vīvit, he lives badly; bene labōrat, he works well; ferē omnēs, almost all; nimis saepe, too often.

Questions

347.
a. A question for information is generally introduced by ‑ne: videtne patrem, does he see his father?b. A question expecting the answer yes is generally introduced by nōnne: nōnne vidēs, don’t you see?c. Moods in Interrogative Sentences

348. The Deliberative Question has its verb in the subjunctive: dīcam an taceam, shall I speak or hold my tongue?; quid facerem, what was I to do?
a. Occasionally the present indicative is used in deliberative questions: quem sequimur, whom are we to follow?
349. The Indirect Question has its verb in the subjunctive: quaerēmus (1) quid fēcerit, (2) quid faciat, (3) quid factūrus sit, we shall ask what he has done, what he is doing, what he is going to do.
350. The direct form of these three questions would be: (1) quid fēcit? (2) quid facit? (3) quid faciet (or factūrus est)?

Tenses

351. There are six tenses in Latin: present, imperfect, future, perfect, pluperfect, and future perfect.
1. The Present denotes continuance in the present: (1) of what is going on now: auribus teneō lupum, I am holding a wolf by the ears; (2) of statements true for all time: probitās laudātur et alget, honesty is praised and freezes.a. The Historical Present. Used far more than in English for lively representation of the past: cohortīs incēdere jubet, he orders the cohorts to advance.b. Used of actions continuing into the present, especially with jam, jam diū, jam prīdem: Mithridātēs annum jam tertium et vīcēsimum rēgnat, Mithridates has been reigning now going on twenty-three years.c. Sometimes used for the future, or for attempted action (conative present): quam prendimus arcem, what citadel are we to seize?; uxōrem dūcis, are you to be married?; quid mē terrēs, why do you try to frighten me?2. The Imperfect denotes continued or repeated action in the past: pugnābam, I was fighting, I kept fighting, I used to fight.a. The Imperfect of Endeavor. Used of attempted, interrupted, intended, or expected actions: urbem relinquēbat, he was trying to leave the city; lēx abrogābātur, the law was to be abrogated.b. The imperfect dwells on the process; the historical perfect states the result. The two supplement each other.3. The Future denotes continuance in the future (scrībam, I shall be writing), and also indefinite future action (scrībam, I shall write).a. The future is sometimes used in an imperative sense, as in English: cum volet, accēdēs; cum tē vītābit, abībis, when she wants you, approach; when she avoids you, be off with you. Compare “Thou shalt not kill.”b. A similar use is the future in asseverations: ita mē amābit Juppiter, so help me, Jove!4. The Perfect has two distinct uses: the pure perfect and the historical perfect.a. The Pure Perfect (present perfect) expresses completion in the present, of an action now over: vīximus, we have lived; Trōja fuit, Troy has been (but is no longer). More often it denotes the present result of a remoter action: āctum est, perīstī, it is all over, you are ruined; equum et mūlum Brundisiī tibi relīquī, I have left a horse and mule for you at Brundisium.b. The Historical (Indefinite) Perfect states a past action simply as a thing attained: vēnī, vīdī, vīcī, I came, I saw, I conquered; Milō domum vēnit, calceōs et vestīmenta mūtāvit, paulisper commorātus est, Milo came home, changed shoes and clothes, tarried a little while.5. The Pluperfect denotes completion in the past, of an action completed before another began: fuerat inimīcus, he had been my enemy.6. The Future Perfect denotes both completion and attainment: fēcerō, I shall have done it, or I shall do it (once and for all).
352. The Latin tenses are divided into primary (principal) and secondary (historical).
a. Primary Tenses have to do with present and future: present, pure perfect, future, and future perfect.b. Secondary Tenses have to do with the past: historical present, imperfect, historical perfect, and pluperfect.

Moods in Subordinate Sentences

Sequence of Tenses

Reading the sequence rule. “Sequence of tenses” just means the tense of the main verb steers the tense of the subjunctive in the clause depending on it — the same instinct that makes English say he says he is tired but he said he was tired. The grid below sorts Latin’s main verbs into two groups (primary and secondary) and matches each to two subjunctive tenses, according to whether the dependent action is still going on or already finished.
353. Primary tenses are ordinarily followed by primary tenses, secondary by secondary, as follows:
Primary (present & future)are followed bythe present subjunctive (continued action); the perfect subjunctive (completed action).
Secondary (past)are followed bythe imperfect subjunctive (continued action); the pluperfect subjunctive (completed action).
a. An action completed with regard to the leading verb may itself be continued. Latin cannot make this distinction, so the imperfect indicative is represented in the dependent form by the perfect or pluperfect: nesciō quid fēcerit, nescīvī quid fēcisset.b. The rule is subject to several modifications: (1) tense refers to time, not merely tense form; (2) the effect of a past action may continue into the present or future; (3) a dependent clause may follow a varying sequence; (4) an original imperfect or pluperfect subjunctive does not change its tense even when depending on a primary verb (382, a).
354. The future relation of a verb in a dependent subjunctive clause may be made clearer by an active periphrastic: cognōscam, quid factūrus sīs, I shall try to find out what you are going to do; cognōvī quid factūrus essēs, I have found out what you were going to do.
355. In Indirect Discourse all verbs in subordinate clauses are in the subjunctive and follow the general rule of sequence of tenses. see 390.

Quod Clauses

356. Quod the fact that, in that is used with the indicative to introduce explanatory clauses after verbs of adding and dropping, doing and happening, and after demonstrative expressions: adde quod vīcimus, add the fact that we have won; praetereō quod nōn mānsit, I pass over the fact that he did not remain; bene facis quod tacēs, you do well in that you keep silent.

Causal Clauses

A causal clause gives the reason or cause for the main clause — the ‘because’ clause.
357. Quod, quia, quoniam, and quandō take the indicative in direct discourse, the subjunctive in implied indirect discourse, to express cause: fūgit quod timēbat, he fled because he was afraid; fūgit quod timēret, he fled because (as he said) he was afraid.
358. Quod is used after verbs of emotion with the indicative (direct) or subjunctive (implied indirect) to give the reason: gaudet quod vēnērunt, he rejoices that they have come; gaudet quod vēnerint, he rejoices that (as he says) they have come.

Purpose Clauses

359. Purpose is expressed by the present or imperfect subjunctive with ut or nē: vēnit ut dūceret hominēs, he came to lead the men; fugit nē capiātur, he flees lest he be captured. For relative clauses of purpose, see 388.
360. Substantive Volitive Clauses. The present or imperfect subjunctive with ut or nē is used in a substantive clause after verbs of will and desire: ōrō ut veniās, I beg that you come; volō ut veniat, I wish him to come; voluī nē venīret, I wished him not to come.
a. The infinitive may also be used with such verbs, especially in poetry.b. Jubeō regularly takes the accusative and infinitive.

Verbs of Hindering

These are verbs meaning to prevent, hinder, refuse, or stand in the way.
361. A verb of preventing, refusing, and the like may take nē or quōminus with the subjunctive: obstat nē veniat, he hinders him from coming.
362. A negatived verb of preventing may take quīn or quōminus with the subjunctive: nihil obstat quīn (or quōminus) sīs beātus, nothing hinders you from being happy.

Verbs of Fearing

These are verbs meaning to fear, dread, or be afraid.
363. A verb of fear may be followed by nē or ut (= nē + nōn) with any tense of the subjunctive: timeō nē hostis veniat, I fear lest the enemy come; timeō nē hostis vēnerit, I fear lest the enemy have come; timeō ut amīcus veniat, I fear lest my friend come not. The polarity makes sense from the origins: a purpose clause grows from a command — ut = “let it happen!” (the thing you want) — but a fear clause grows from “let it NOT happen, I’m afraid!” So with fearing verbs nē marks the thing feared (that it WILL come) and ut marks that it won’t.

Result Clauses

364. The subjunctive with ut or ut nōn denotes result: tanta vīs deōrum est ut eīs nōn possīmus obstāre, so great is the power of the gods that we cannot oppose them; nēmō est tam fortis ut nōn possit cadere, no one is so strong but he can fall.
a. A relative pronoun is sometimes used instead of ut: nēmō est tam fortis quī nōn possit cadere, no one is so strong but he can fall.
365. A verb of effecting has the subjunctive with ut, nē, or ut nōn: faciam ut veniat, I shall make him come; faciam nē veniat, I shall bring it about that he does not come.
366. Negatived or questioned verbs of doubt and uncertainty may be followed by the subjunctive with quīn: nōn dubium est quīn urbs capiātur, there is no doubt that the city is being captured.
367. Substantive Clauses of Result. The subjunctive with ut is often used in a substantive clause to give the contents or character of a preceding substantive, adjective, or pronoun: tōtum in eō est, ut tibi imperēs, all depends on this, your self-command; id est proprium cīvitātis, ut sit lībera, this is the peculiar privilege of a state, to be free.

Temporal Clauses

A temporal clause tells when the main action happens.
368. Ut, ut prīmum, cum, cum prīmum, ubi, ubi prīmum, simul ac, simul atque, in the sense as soon as, and postquam take the perfect indicative: postquam vēnit dux, urbs capta est, after the leader came, the city was captured; ut vīdit urbem captam, fūgit, as soon as he saw the city captured, he fled.
a. The imperfect indicative is used of overlapping action, the pluperfect when a definite interval is given: ut vidēbat hostēs vincentēs, rediit domum, as he saw the enemy were conquering, he returned home; postquam cēperat urbem, mīlitēs dūcit domum, after he had captured the city, he led his soldiers home.
369. When two repeated actions are contemporaneous, both are in the indicative in tenses of continuance: rēx bellum gerēbat cum volēbat, the king waged war whenever he wished.
370. When one repeated action comes before another, the antecedent action is in the perfect, pluperfect, or future perfect indicative, the subsequent in the present, imperfect, or future: quotiēns cecidit, surgit, as often as he falls (has fallen), he rises; quotiēns ceciderat, surgēbat, as often as he fell, he rose; quotiēns ceciderit, surget, as often as he falls, he will rise.
371. Dum, dōnec, quoad, and quamdiū so long as, while take the indicative of all tenses: vīta dum superest, bene est, while there is life, it is well; dōnec grātus eram tibi, fēlīx fuī, while I was pleasing to you, I was fortunate; quoad potuit restitit, he resisted as long as he could.
372. Dum while, while yet usually takes the present indicative when the main verb is in a past tense: dum haec rēs aguntur, urbs ārdēbat, while these things were going on, the city was burning.
373. Dum, dōnec, and quoad until take the present, historical present, historical perfect, or future perfect indicative when suspense or anticipation are not involved: manēbō dum venit, I shall remain until he comes; mānsī dum (quoad, dōnec) vēnit, I remained until he came. see 374.
374. Dum, dōnec, and quoad until take the subjunctive when suspense, anticipation, or design is involved: mānsī dum venīret, I remained until he could come; exspectō dum veniat, I am waiting for him to come. see 373.
375. Dum, modo, and dummodo if only, provided only take the present or imperfect subjunctive in a proviso clause: ōderint dum metuant, let them hate so long as they fear; veniat, dum maneat, let him come, provided that he remains.
376. Antequam and priusquam take the indicative (present, perfect, or future perfect) when the time limit is stated as fact: antequam abeō, dīcam pauca, before I go, I shall say a few words; antequam abiit, dīxerat pauca, before he went, he had said a few words.
a. Antequam and priusquam take the subjunctive when the action is anticipated, contingent, or designed: urbem capit priusquam rēx veniat, he captures the city before the king may arrive; ante vidēmus fulgōrem quam sonum audiāmus, we see the flash of lightning before we hear the sound.

Cum Clauses

377. Temporal cum when is used with all tenses of the indicative for merely temporal relations: animus nec cum adest nec cum discēdit appāret, the soul is not visible, either when it is present or when it departs; pāruit cum necesse erat, he obeyed when it was necessary.
378. Descriptive cum when is used with the imperfect and pluperfect subjunctive to give circumstances: cum dīmicāret, occīsus est, when he engaged in battle, he was slain; Caesar cum id nūntiātum esset, mātūrat ab urbe proficīscī, when this was announced to Caesar, he hastened to set out from the city. Sometimes called cum circumstantial.
379. Causal or Concessive cum when, whereas, although is used with any tense of the subjunctive: quae cum ita sint, dēbet fugere, since these things are so, we must flee; cum pār esset armīs, tamen dēbuerat fugere, although he was equal in arms, nevertheless he had to flee.

A conditional sentence is an ‘if… then…’ sentence — an ‘if’ clause (the protasis) stating a condition and a main clause (the apodosis) stating the result.

383. Ut sī, ac sī, quasi, quam sī, tamquam, tamquam sī, velut, or velut sī with the subjunctive are used in a clause of comparison: tantus metus patrēs cēpit, velut sī jam ad portās hostis esset, a great fear took hold of the senators, as if the enemy were already at the gates.

Concessive Clauses

384. A Concessive Clause (a subordinate clause conceding a contrasting point, the ‘although’ clause) may be introduced by etsī, etiamsī, or tametsī with the indicative or subjunctive; by quamquam with the indicative only; by quamvīs with the subjunctive only.

Relative Clauses

385. The Relative Clause as such (used as an adjective to modify a noun) regularly has its verb in the indicative: amō virum quī fortis est, I like a man who is brave.
386. An indefinite or general relative clause usually has its verb in the indicative; so explanatory quī, when equivalent to quod: errāverim fortasse, quī mē aliquid putāvī, I may have erred in thinking myself to be something.
387. A relative clause that depends on an infinitive or subjunctive and forms an integral part of the thought has its verb in the subjunctive by attraction: pigrī est ingeniī contentum esse iīs quae sint ab aliīs inventa, it is the mark of a lazy mind to be content with what has been found out by others.
388. A relative clause has its verb in the subjunctive when quī is equivalent to ut is in an expression of purpose: ēripiunt aliīs quod aliīs dent, they snatch from some to give to others. This is the relative clause of purpose.
389. A relative clause has its verb in the subjunctive when quī is equivalent to ut is in a clause of description or characteristic; so after an indefinite antecedent and after dignus, indignus, idōneus, aptus, etc.: multī sunt quī ēripiant, there are many to snatch away; sunt (eī) quī dīcant, there are those who say; dignus est quī cōnsul fīat, he is worthy of being made consul. This is the subjunctive of characteristic.

Indirect Discourse

390. Indirect discourse (indirect statement), as opposed to direct discourse, gives the main drift of a speech, not the exact words.
a. Indirect discourse depends on some verb of saying, showing, believing, perceiving, or thinking, expressed or implied.b. In indirect discourse a principal statement has its verb in the infinitive: dīcit eōs vēnisse, he says that they have come; dīcit eōs venīre, he says that they are coming; a question or command has its verb in the subjunctive: quaerit quid velint, he asks what they want; quaesīvit quid vellent, he asked what they wanted; dīcit hominibus ut veniant, he tells the men to come; dīxit hominibus ut fugerent, he told the men to flee.c. A subordinate clause in indirect discourse has its verb in the subjunctive. see 355.

Versification

The Dactylic Hexameter

391. One of the most common meters of Latin poetry is the dactylic hexameter. It was commonly employed by the Greeks and Romans in epic (narrative) poetry — the Iliad and Odyssey of Homer, the Aeneid of Vergil, the Metamorphoses of Ovid. It is occasionally used in English, as in Longfellow’s Evangeline.
392. There are six feet (a foot being the basic rhythmic unit of a verse line, a set pattern of long and short syllables) in a hexameter verse. The first five feet are either dactyls (one long syllable followed by two shorts, ) or spondees (two longs, ). The sixth foot is always treated as a spondee ().
393. The final syllable of a hexameter verse may be either long or short (syllaba anceps); but in scansion (the analysis of a verse line to mark its rhythm, finding which syllables are long or short and dividing the line into feet) it is counted long and so marked.
394. The Metrical Scheme of a verse is thus. Each foot opens with a long syllable (bearing the metrical accent, marked ′); its second half resolves either as two shorts (a dactyl, upper tier) or as one long (a spondee, lower tier). The final foot shows the brevis in longō — its last position is in fact often short (upper tier) but is counted long in scansion (lower tier):

That is, the first syllable of each foot must be long. It is also given slightly more stress than the other half of the foot; this stress is the metrical accent. (see 407 below).
a. A short syllable is often lengthened under the metrical accent or before a pause.
The so-called "ictus" or "Metrical Accent" In Mr. Hotchkiss's opinion, the "Metrical Accent" is a matter of rhythmic prominence, like the relative strength of the different positions in a measure of music. Giving it "slightly more stress" like Pharr suggests can be helpful in clarifying the structure, but the goal is to appreciate the music that arises naturally from contrasts in length (cf. 407).
395. The fifth foot is almost always a dactyl. When a spondee is used there, the verse moves more slowly and is called spondaic.
396. For metrical purposes each syllable is long or short, a long occupying about twice the time of a short (14). Hence a spondaic foot () is the metrical equivalent of a dactylic foot ().
397. Only long vowels are marked in the Latin of this book. For the rules on the length of syllables, see 14–24.
398. Elision. Whenever a word ends with a vowel, diphthong, or m, and the following word begins with a vowel or h, the first vowel or diphthong is regularly elided — not omitted entirely, but lightly and hurriedly half-pronounced, somewhat like grace notes in music.
399. Hiatus. Occasionally a word ending in a vowel, diphthong, or m is followed by a word beginning with a vowel or h and elision does not take place. This is called hiatus.
400. Semi-hiatus. Sometimes when a word ends in a long vowel or diphthong and the next word begins with a vowel or h, the long final vowel or diphthong is shortened. This is called semi-hiatus.
401. The vowels i and u are sometimes used as consonants (j, v): abiete (pronounce abjete), genua (pronounce genva).
402. Hypermeter. Sometimes the final syllable of a verse is elided before a vowel beginning the next verse. This is called synapheia, and the verse whose final syllable is elided is a hypermeter or hypermetric verse.
403. Synizesis. Two successive vowels that do not ordinarily form a diphthong are sometimes pronounced as one syllable for the meter. This contraction within a word is called synizesis.

Hints on the Metrical Reading of Latin Poetry

405. In reading Latin poetry aloud, keep the words distinct and make no break between separate feet unless there is a pause in sense.
406. Pay careful attention to meaning, and indicate the pauses in sense by the voice. Do not let the voice drop at the end of a verse unless there is a distinct pause in sense.
407. Remember that the rhythm of Latin verse is based primarily on the regular succession of long and short syllables; that of English, on accented and unaccented syllables.
408. To gain facility, memorize a good deal of verse, paying special attention to quantity — about twice as much time for each long syllable as for a short.
409. Caesura.footnote Whenever a word ends within a foot, the break is called caesura. If it coincides with a pause in the verse, it is the principal caesura (or simply the caesura of the verse). Mr. Hotchkiss has different notions about what makes the principal caesura "principal." Fortunately, he more or less agrees with Pharr's note below, so it doesn't make much difference.
410. A single verse may have more than one caesura, each marked by a pause in sense. The principal caesuras are: (1) after the first long syllable of the third foot; (2) after the first long syllable of the fourth foot.
In the teaching of scansion to beginners, it is preferable to omit all reference to caesura for at least the first two or three years of reading Latin poetry. These paragraphs on caesura are given for the benefit of teachers who may wish their pupils to know something about it.

Figures of Syntax and Rhetoric

411. Alliteration is the repetition of the same letter or sound: tum vīctū revocant vīrīs, then they recall their strength with food (the sound of v).
412. Anacolouthon or want of sequence occurs when the scheme of a sentence is changed in its course.
413. Anaphora is the repetition of a word or words at the beginning of successive clauses: mīrantur dōna Aenēae, mīrantur Iūlum, they marvel at the gifts of Aeneas, they marvel at Iulus.
414. Anastrophe is an inversion of the usual order of words: tē propter, on account of you.
415. Antithesis is an opposition or contrast of words or ideas: speciē blanda, reāpse repudianda, charming in appearance, in reality to be rejected.
416. Aposiopesis is a breaking off before the close of the sentence: quōs ego — sed mōtōs praestat compōnere flūctūs, whom I — but it is better to calm the angry waves.
417. Apostrophe is a sudden break from the previous method of discourse to address, in the second person, some person or object, absent or present: Ō patria, Ō dīvum domus, O fatherland, O home of the gods!
418. Asyndeton is the omission of conjunctions: ancora dē prōrā jacitur; stant lītore puppēs, the anchor is cast from the prow; the sterns rest on the beach.
419. Brachylogy (breviloquentia) is the failure to repeat an element that must be supplied in modified form: tam fēlīx essēs quam fōrmōsissima (es) vellem, would that you were as fortunate as you are fair.
420. Chiasmus is the arrangement of corresponding pairs of words in opposite order: Īlionēa petit dextrā laevāque Serestum, he sought Ilioneus with his right and with his left Serestus.
421. Ellipsis is the omission of words obviously understood but needed to complete the construction: Aeolus haec contrā, Aeolus thus (spoke) in reply. The words most commonly omitted are agō, dīcō, faciō, loquor, and sum.
422. Enallage is a shift from one form to another: vōs (pl.), Ō Calliopē (sg.), precor, I entreat you, O Calliope.
423. Euphemism is the substitution of an agreeable word for one that is harsh or unpleasant: sī quid eī acciderit, if anything should happen to him (i.e., if he should die).
424. Euphony is the effect produced by words or sounds so combined as to please the ear.
425. Hendiadys is the expression of one idea by two nouns joined by a conjunction, instead of a noun with a modifier: vī et armīs, by force of arms; vulgus et multitūdō, the common herd.
426. Hypallage is an interchange in the relations of words: dare classibus Austrōs, to give the winds to the fleet (instead of giving the fleet to the winds).
427. Hyperbaton (Trajection) is the violent displacement of words: per omnīs tē deōs ōrō, by all the gods I beg you.
428. Hyperbole is rhetorical exaggeration: praeruptus aquae mōns, a sheer mountain of water (a great wave of the sea).
429. Hysteron Proteron is a reversal of the natural order: moriāmur et in media arma ruāmus, let us die and rush into the midst of arms.
430. Irony states an apparent fact with the manifest intention of expressing its opposite: scīlicet is superīs labor est, ea cūra quiētōs sollicitat, of course this is work for the gods, this care vexes them in their serenity!
431. Litotes (Understatement) is an expression by which more is meant than meets the ear, especially with the negative: nōn indecōrō pulvere sordidī, soiled with not unbecoming (i.e., glorious) dust.
432. Metaphor is an implied comparison: illa, Trōjae et patriae commūnis Erīnys, she (Helen), the common curse of Troy and of her native land.
433. Metonymy is the substitution of one word for another it suggests: Cerēs, goddess of grain, for grain.
434. Onomatopoeia is the use of words whose sound suggests the sense: magnō cum murmure montis, with a mighty rumbling of the mountain.
435. Oxymoron is the use of words apparently contradictory: cum tacent clāmant, when they are silent they cry out.
436. Paraleipsis (Preterition) is an apparent omission by which a speaker artfully pretends to pass by what he really emphasizes: nōn dīcō tē pecūniās accēpisse; rapīnās tuās omnīs omittō, I do not say that you accepted sums of money; I omit all your acts of rapine.
437. Personification attributes personality to an impersonal thing: aspīrat fortūna labōrī, fortune favors our struggle; fuge crūdēlīs terrās, fuge lītus avārum, flee the cruel lands, flee the greedy shore.
438. Pleonasm is the use of superfluous words: sīc ōre locūta est, thus she spoke with her mouth.
439. Polysyndeton is the use of unnecessary conjunctions: ūnā Eurusque Notusque ruunt crēberque procellīs Āfricus, both the East wind and the South wind and the Southwester rush forth together.
440. Prolepsis (Anticipation) is the use of a word, usually a modifier, before it is logically appropriate: summersās obrue puppīs, overwhelm the sunken ships (i.e., sink and overwhelm the ships).
441. Simile is a figure that asserts an explicit comparison between two different things: pār levibus ventīs volucrīque simillima somnō, like unto the light winds and most resembling winged sleep; velutī cum coorta est sēditiō, just as when a riot has arisen.
442. Synchysis is an interlocked order of words: saevae memorem Jūnōnis īram, fell Juno’s unforgetting hate.
443. Synecdoche is the use of a part for the whole, or the reverse: tēctum, roof, for house; puppis, stern, for ship; mūcrō, point, for sword.
444. Synesis is a construction according to sense rather than grammatical form: pars veniunt, part come.
445. Tmesis is the separation of the parts of a compound word by intervening words: quae mē cumque vocant terrae, whatever the lands that call me (for quaecumque terrae mē vocant).
446. Transferred Epithet is an epithet transferred from the word to which it strictly belongs to another word connected with it in thought: vēlivolum mare, the sail-flying sea (sail-flying properly applies to ships).
447. Zeugma is the joining of two words with a modifying or governing word that strictly applies to only one of them: Danaōs et laxat claustra, he looses the barriers and (sets free) the Greeks.
